# Pad Thai

## Ingredients

- 8 oz — flat rice noodles (medium or wide)
- 3 tablespoons — tamarind puree (dissolved from pulp; not concentrate)
- 2 tablespoons — fish sauce
- 1.5 tablespoons — palm sugar (or brown sugar)
- 1 tablespoon — oyster sauce *(optional — adds depth, omit for a purer version)*
- 3 tablespoons — vegetable oil
- 3 — garlic cloves, minced
- 2 — shallots, thinly sliced
- 8 oz — protein (shrimp, chicken, or tofu)
- 2 — eggs
- 1 cup — bean sprouts
- 3 — garlic chives (or green onions), cut into 1-inch pieces
- 1/4 cup — roasted peanuts, crushed (garnish only)
- 1 teaspoon — dried shrimp powder *(optional — builds deeper umami)*
- 2 tablespoons — preserved salted radish, rinsed and chopped *(optional — adds authentic texture)*
- 1-2 — dried red chili, crushed (served at table)
- 1 — lime, cut into wedges (served at table)

## Instructions

1. Mix the sauce before anything else: whisk together tamarind puree, fish sauce, palm sugar, and oyster sauce (if using) in a small bowl. Taste — it should be sour-forward, then salty, then lightly sweet. Adjust as needed and set aside.
2. Soak 8 oz flat rice noodles in room temperature water for 5–20 minutes (depending on thickness) until just pliable but not fully soft. Drain immediately and rinse under cold water to halt cooking and prevent sticking.
3. Heat 3 tablespoons oil in a large wok or skillet over high heat until shimmering.
4. Add minced garlic and shallots; stir-fry for 30 seconds until fragrant.
5. Add protein and cook for 2–3 minutes until nearly cooked through. Add dried shrimp powder and preserved radish (if using) and toss to combine.
6. Push everything to the side of the wok. Crack 2 eggs into the cleared space and scramble until 70% cooked — still slightly wet. Fold the eggs into the protein; residual heat will finish them, keeping them silky.
7. Add the drained noodles and toss continuously for 1–2 minutes.
8. Pour the pre-mixed sauce over the noodles and toss well for 1 minute until evenly coated.
9. Add bean sprouts and garlic chives in the last 30 seconds, tossing briefly to preserve their crunch.
10. Divide among serving plates. Top with crushed peanuts only at this stage — never cook them into the dish.
11. Serve immediately with lime wedges, dried chili flakes, and extra peanuts on the side.

## Chef's Tips

**On tamarind:** Always use tamarind puree made from pulp dissolved in warm water — not tamarind concentrate, which is far too intense and can make the sauce bitter. If tamarind is unavailable, lime juice is a common Western substitute, but authentic Pad Thai relies exclusively on tamarind.

**On eggs:** Fold them in when 70% cooked. The residual heat from the noodles finishes them perfectly and keeps the texture silky rather than rubbery. This is the single most impactful technique tip from professional sources.

**On noodle soaking:** Soak time depends on noodle thickness. Thin noodles need as little as 5 minutes; medium and wide noodles may need up to 20. The target is pliable, not soft — they will finish cooking in the wok. Always rinse under cold water immediately after draining.

**On peanuts:** Crush and add as garnish only, never cooked into the dish. Cooking them turns them soggy and mutes their flavor.

**On batch size:** Do not crowd the pan. If doubling the recipe, cook in two separate batches. Crowding drops wok temperature, causing the noodles to steam rather than stir-fry.

**Controversy — preserved radish and dried shrimp:** Authentic Thai recipes include both for layered umami. Most Western adaptations skip them for accessibility. They are marked optional here but are strongly recommended if you want street-food depth.

**Controversy — oyster sauce:** Purists omit it as non-traditional; many restaurant-quality Western recipes include a small amount for added sweetness and body. The small quantity used here does not overwhelm the dish and is a reasonable compromise.

**Controversy — palm sugar vs. brown sugar:** Palm sugar is traditional and has a gentle caramel note. Brown sugar or muscovado are reliable substitutes with minimal flavor difference in the final dish.
