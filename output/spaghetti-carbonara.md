# Spaghetti Carbonara

## Ingredients
- 400g — spaghetti
- 150g — guanciale (see Controversies note below)
- 2 whole eggs + 2 extra yolks (see note below)
- 60g — Pecorino Romano, freshly grated
- 40g — Parmigiano-Reggiano, freshly grated
- 2 tsp — coarsely ground black pepper
- 1 splash — dry white wine (optional, for deglazing)
- to taste — salt (for pasta water only)

## Instructions
1. Bring a large pot of heavily salted water to a boil and cook spaghetti until al dente. Reserve at least 1 cup of pasta cooking water before draining.
2. While pasta cooks, cut guanciale into small cubes or lardons and render in a large skillet over medium-low heat until the fat is released and the edges are lightly golden but not fully crispy, about 5–7 minutes. You want some soft rendered fat remaining — it is essential to the sauce.
3. Optional: deglaze the pan with a splash of dry white wine, scraping up any browned bits. Allow the alcohol to cook off for 1 minute.
4. In a bowl, whisk together 2 whole eggs and 2 extra yolks with the grated Pecorino Romano, Parmigiano-Reggiano, and plenty of coarsely ground black pepper until fully combined into a single, uniform mixture.
5. Pre-warm your serving bowls by filling them with hot water; set aside and empty just before serving.
6. Remove the skillet from the heat entirely. Add the hot drained pasta to the skillet and toss to coat in the guanciale fat.
7. With the pan still off the heat, quickly pour in the egg-and-cheese mixture, using tongs to lift and fold the pasta constantly so every strand is coated. The residual heat — not direct flame — cooks the eggs gently without scrambling them.
8. Add reserved pasta water one tablespoon at a time, tossing continuously, until the sauce is silky, glossy, and emulsified. This usually takes 3–5 tablespoons.
9. Stir in the cooked guanciale and adjust seasoning with black pepper. Do not add salt — the cured meat and aged cheeses provide plenty.
10. Immediately divide into the pre-warmed bowls and finish with a generous crack of black pepper and extra grated Pecorino Romano.

## Chef's Tips
- **Extra yolks are non-negotiable for stability.** A 2 whole eggs + 2 yolks ratio gives a richer, more emulsified sauce that is far less likely to scramble or seize than whole-egg-only recipes.
- **Off the heat is the most important rule.** The egg mixture must never touch an active burner. Residual heat from the pasta and pan is exactly the right temperature.
- **Pasta water is your emulsifier.** Starchy cooking water — added gradually, one tablespoon at a time — is what transforms eggs and fat into a creamy sauce. Never use plain water.
- **Pre-warming the bowl buys time.** Carbonara tightens fast as it cools. Warm bowls give you an extra minute to get the sauce to the table at its best.
- **Do not let the guanciale go fully crispy.** Some soft, rendered fat in the pan is essential for coating the pasta before the egg mixture goes in. If it is all crunch and no fat, the sauce will be dry.
- **Grate the cheese very finely.** Coarsely grated cheese will clump rather than melt seamlessly into the sauce.
- **If the sauce tightens before serving,** add a small splash of hot reserved pasta water and toss vigorously to loosen it.

## Controversies

**Guanciale vs. pancetta vs. bacon:** Roman tradition is unambiguous — guanciale (cured pork cheek) is the correct choice. Its higher fat ratio and distinct flavour cannot be replicated by pancetta. Bacon is a firm no. This recipe uses guanciale.

**Whole eggs vs. yolks only:** Some Roman purists use only yolks; many authoritative sources use a mix. The 2 whole eggs + 2 yolks approach (per NYT Cooking) is the best practical compromise — yolks provide richness and stability, the whites provide enough liquid to loosen the sauce without added water.

**Cheese blend vs. Pecorino only:** Strict Roman orthodoxy calls for 100% Pecorino Romano. However, a 50/50 blend with Parmigiano-Reggiano (as used by BBC Good Food and Marcella Hazan) produces a more balanced, less aggressively salty result. This recipe uses a 60/40 split in favour of Pecorino to keep the Roman character while improving balance.

**Cream:** Completely absent from every authoritative source consulted. Not included.

**Garlic:** Not present in traditional Roman carbonara. Hazan uses garlic-infused oil as a personal variation. This recipe omits it from the main method but you may use it as an optional variation.
